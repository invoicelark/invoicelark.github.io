Photographer Invoice Template

Photography invoices need to separate three different things clients constantly conflate: your time on the shoot, the editing hours afterwards, and the license to use the images. Put them on separate lines and disputes about "but I thought I owned the photos" mostly disappear.

Typical photographer rates

$100–$300/hour for commercial work; day rates $800–$3,000. Rates vary by market and experience — if you're unsure where you sit, run your numbers through our hourly rate calculator.

Billing tip for photographers

Always invoice the usage license as its own line. It anchors the value of the images beyond your hours and creates a natural upsell when the client wants broader usage later.

Tired of chasing payments? Put your payment terms and a late-fee clause on every invoice (our late fee calculator shows what an overdue balance actually costs your client), and make paying you as frictionless as possible.

What every photographer invoice needs

  1. A unique invoice number and issue date
  2. Your business name, contact details (and tax/license ID where required)
  3. The client's name and billing details
  4. Clear line items — like the examples above — with quantities and rates
  5. Subtotal, any tax, and the total due
  6. Payment terms: due date, accepted methods, and your late-fee policy
