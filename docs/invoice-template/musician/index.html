Musician Invoice Template

Gig invoices should read like a run sheet: date, venue, set times, and what's included (PA, lighting, learning requests). Studio work is different — there you're invoicing per song or per hour plus usage, and the two should never be mixed on one line.

Typical musician rates

$200–$1,500 per gig; session work $50–$150/hour or $100–$400/song. Rates vary by market and experience — if you're unsure where you sit, run your numbers through our hourly rate calculator.

Billing tip for musicians

Require the balance before you play, not after — collecting from a venue manager at 1 a.m. after load-out is nobody's best negotiating position.

Tired of chasing payments? Put your payment terms and a late-fee clause on every invoice (our late fee calculator shows what an overdue balance actually costs your client), and make paying you as frictionless as possible.

What every musician invoice needs

  1. A unique invoice number and issue date
  2. Your business name, contact details (and tax/license ID where required)
  3. The client's name and billing details
  4. Clear line items — like the examples above — with quantities and rates
  5. Subtotal, any tax, and the total due
  6. Payment terms: due date, accepted methods, and your late-fee policy
