DJ Invoice Template

DJ invoices sell a covered date plus equipment plus hours, and overtime is where the money leaks: state the booked window and the overtime rate on the invoice, because 'one more hour' at midnight is a decision made in the worst negotiating conditions of your life.

Typical dj rates

$500–$2,500 per event; corporate rates higher. Rates vary by market and experience — if you're unsure where you sit, run your numbers through our hourly rate calculator.

Billing tip for djs

Print the overtime rate on the invoice before the event. When the dance floor is full, the client will happily agree to it — the mistake is negotiating it then instead of documenting it now.

Tired of chasing payments? Put your payment terms and a late-fee clause on every invoice (our late fee calculator shows what an overdue balance actually costs your client), and make paying you as frictionless as possible.

What every dj invoice needs

  1. A unique invoice number and issue date
  2. Your business name, contact details (and tax/license ID where required)
  3. The client's name and billing details
  4. Clear line items — like the examples above — with quantities and rates
  5. Subtotal, any tax, and the total due
  6. Payment terms: due date, accepted methods, and your late-fee policy
