Writing invoices work best when each line is a finished asset with its word count and rounds included — and when kill fees and usage are already in the terms. Publications pay on invoice receipt dates, so send the invoice the day the piece is accepted, not published.

Typical freelance writer rates

$0.10–$1.50 per word; $50–$200/hour; blog retainers $800–$4,000/month. Rates vary by market and experience — if you're unsure where you sit, run your numbers through our hourly rate calculator.

Billing tip for freelance writers

Invoice on acceptance, not publication — editorial calendars slip by months and your rent doesn't. Any publication worth writing for pays on acceptance if you ask.

Tired of chasing payments? Put your payment terms and a late-fee clause on every invoice (our late fee calculator shows what an overdue balance actually costs your client), and make paying you as frictionless as possible.

What every freelance writer invoice needs

  1. A unique invoice number and issue date
  2. Your business name, contact details (and tax/license ID where required)
  3. The client's name and billing details
  4. Clear line items — like the examples above — with quantities and rates
  5. Subtotal, any tax, and the total due
  6. Payment terms: due date, accepted methods, and your late-fee policy
